]]> This is often very confusing to decide what type of water purifier do you really need especially when you see a lot of varieties in the market. Ecommerce is already flooded with RO purifiers but, do you really need them for your home? Let’s find out!

Measuring TDS and why it’s important?

Total Dissolved Solids (TDS) is a measure of all organic and inorganic substances in water such as soluble salts and heavy metal ions. TDS reading will help you determine the quality of water and decide what type of purifier you may need.

To measure TDS, dip the TDS water tester in a water sample from your water supply and match the reading with this WHO rating chart. I have also added a reference to one of the reliable TDS tester which you can buy.

Before concluding anything, let’s quickly understand different terms and types of water purifiers and when you need them.

Purpose of UV and UF in water treatment

Ultraviolet (UV) is a form of electromagnetic radiation and invisible to the human eye. It is effective against biological impurities in water such as bacteria and viruses. Viruses are about 100 times smaller than bacteria and their size varies from 0.004 to 0.1 microns.

You may have noticed that many water purifiers have UV lamp as an added measure, usually at last stage of purification to eliminate chances of any such impurities from water.

On the other hand, Ultrafiltration (UF) membranes are used to remove impurities such as muddy sediments, soluble salts, and some bacteria/viruses in large micron size. Usually, UF membranes come with a filter size of ~0.01 micron and it may vary according to the manufacturer.

UF membrane filter of 0.01-micron filter impurities greater than or equal to 0.01 micron. This type of membrane requires adequate pressure for filtration.

Gravity water purifier

This type of purifier uses the advantage of gravity for water flow in different stages of purification. Most manufacturers use fiber mesh, activated carbon, and related UF membranes for filtration up to 0.01 micron.

RO Water Purifier

RO (Reverse Osmosis) is originally used in an investigation for desalination. Many applications for RO are developed later. Unfortunately, due to misleading marketing and lack of awareness, many Indian households use RO water purifiers even if they don’t need them.

The following table will help you to decide ‘What purifier should I buy?’ and understand your water purification requirements.

FAQs based on the following scenario

Muddy water with TDS of 600 ppm

For TDS greater than 300 ppm, you need RO for effective filtration. As water is muddy, you also need a UF membrane. Thus, a water purifier with UF + RO is suitable for this scenario.

Municipality-treated water supply with a TDS of 30 ppm

In this scenario, TDS of water is less than 300 ppm and municipality treated water does not contain large micron impurities such as mud. So, a Gravity water purifier is most suitable in this scenario.

]]> Every household produces a generous amount of kitchen waste daily, which can be reused for composting, to get utilized as fertilizer for your plants but, most of us end up wasting “kitchen/vegetable waste”. If you often require fertilizer for your plants, this post will guide you to make your own compost at home!

This guide will walk you through a fail-proof method to prepare compost from kitchen waste within one week and fortunately doesn’t require huge investment, in comparison to composter bins available in the market.

1. Identify waste and Prepare your collection bin

Before you begin, it is really important for you to understand what type of kitchen waste is suitable for compost and you have probably heard about to not put acidic foods such as lemon, or non-vegetarian stuff such as bones or meat.

A few of the above-mentioned rules are not applicable for this method because we are not using worms to decompose kitchen waste. But, I suggest you filter/pick whatever is easy to grind and put everything in your collection bin, except dairy, non-vegetarian and non-biodegradable stuff!

Put some layer of old newspaper/dry leaves at the bottom of your bin to soak excess liquid from waste and prevent bad smell, and please make sure to put dry waste or with minimum moisture only.

2. Prepare to grind collected waste

This step is added to accelerate the decomposition of collected waste by grinding them into a fine and thick paste. It may sound gross but, it will speed up the process by 10-15x.

2.1 Wear a mask, cover your nose and put your gloves

As you’ve been collecting waste for over a few days. Your bin may develop a strong odor. Thus, It is advised and important to wear a mask or cover your nose to prevent inhaling strong and pungent odors from your bin that may give you a headache or nausea (depend on how strong it is).

Before you begin with the next step, I suggest you do this activity outdoor or with proper ventilation to prevent the spread of any strong odor from the waste collected in your bin.

2.2 Grind waste into thick paste

If you have a large mixer/grinder Jar to spare, you can make use of it to prepare the paste. Otherwise, you have to arrange one and it doesn’t cost much.

Once, you have it ready, grind some portion of your kitchen waste in a grinder/blender with little water and in just a few seconds, your paste is ready, and repeat this process for the whole bin.

3. Mix the thick blended paste with soil properly

Now, your job is half done. It is crucial to mix the paste with clean and fine soil properly. You can do this with the help of a shovel or with your hands. It’s up to you, the way you are comfortable with.

Soil that does not have existing/dead roots, litter, etc. is termed as clean soil here and it is advised to use clean soil to prevent any root to revive during composting, retain all nutrients and help microbes work effectively.

The soil mix should not form thick clumps and its consistency should always be close to ~20-30% moist. Now, your soil mix is ready for composting.

4. Prepare Compost Bin

If you can afford to buy a compost bin, then you can purchase according to your requirement. However, they are expensive and serve the same purpose as home-made bins.

To make a compost bin at home, you need to medium to large size bucket with a lid to cover and retain heat. Now, you need to make some small holes (2-3 mm) on the sides of your bucket to maintain airflow in the compost bin. Don’t put holes at the bottom/top of the bucket.

Now, put your soil mix into this bin and leave it outdoor under the sun and give your soil mix a nice stir every day. After a week, dry the compost under the sun for some time (~1-2 hours).

This method is tried and tested at home by myself and has no chance of failure if you follow all steps as described. It will provide you home-made compost within a week which is 10-15 times faster than other traditional methods.

Avoid oil that is refined, hydrogenated, or contains Trans-fat!

Unfortunately, most of the popular oil/ghee brands in the Indian market are processed and contains trans-fat.

Refined oil is one of the popular choices among Indians to cook food. It is sad but true! Refined/Hydrogenated oil is highly processed with chemicals and sometimes contains a high amount of trans-fatty acids.

I never came across any evidence/study which proves that the use of refined oil in your meal may increase your risk of chronic diseases, including diabetes, heart-attack, blood-pressure, etc. But, it is certain that Trans Fatty acids and Saturated Fat in a high amount will do severe damage to your body!

I have considered one of the most popular refined oil used in India as an example to demonstrate a High Amount of Trans and Saturated Fat in refined oil.

If you continue to use an oil with a high amount of bad fats, you may encounter chronic illness in your life soon. You should not pursue the habit of using refined oil in your diet due to its affordability at all!

Oil is an important source of energy in an Indian diet and we should always evaluate our food choices for better health and fortune!

Choose Regional Cold pressed Oil, as an alternative to refined oil!

Always consider wood/cold-pressed oil which is easily available in your region.

In North/North-East, Mustard Oil is easily available and often termed as Kachhi Ghani Sarso/Mustard Oil ⁜

In Rajasthan, Andhra Pradesh, and Telangana, Sesame Oil ⁜ is one of the popular consumer choices of cold-pressed oil.

In the middle states of India such as Gujarat, etc., Peanut oil ⁜ is considered one of the good sources of healthy fat in the diet.

In South India, Coconut oil ⁜ is a popular wood-pressed oil choice and it perfectly blends with the regional environment as well!

Don’t fall into the trap of innovative advertisements and always prefer local/regional unprocessed food choices over modern processed food!

]]> Modern cookware always allures us and increase our temptation to bring them into the kitchen. As demand increases, many manufacturers started introducing non-stick and ceramic cookware into the market because they can easily cook food with less oil and dish-washing become total bliss.

But, as you know, with great power comes great responsibility. If you read handling instructions or warnings from the product description, it has clearly mentioned the hassle that no one goes through!

Hence, you will pay the price for your own health. Let me explain, what exactly does it mean and how does it really matter?!

Don’t let the beauty of coating allure you!

Non-stick cookware looks beautiful and has a smooth/glossy coat on metal which makes it look premium, but, it has adverse effects on the environment and your health as well.

These products often label a few warnings and hidden dangers that many consumers often overlook! such as Teflon, this coating is unstable at higher temperatures and releases toxic fumes.

Any coating such as Teflon or Ceramic, on metal is very thin and less durable. It is more likely to leech chemicals into your food once the coating starts to wear off with time and usage. So, it’s better to avoid them!

Try to distance yourself from Aluminum!

Aluminum found its way to most Indian households because it is the most abundant metal on earth and is inexpensive. When you cook in aluminum cookware, it leeches into your food easily and it’s highly reactive to acidic foods such as lemon, tomato, etc.

Various studies also suggest that long-term use of aluminum poses a threat to the brain, kidneys, and other vital organs, even under the recommended limit, and it is often linked to Alzheimer’s disease.

According to Ayurveda

1. Cook in Brass and Copper for high metabolic rate

Copper-rich food helps in increasing the metabolic rate of the body and collagen. Brass was a common choice among people of small towns and villages. They are useful in detoxification, improving hemoglobin, and to aid digestion.

But, Brass and Copper’s cookware require tinning every 2-3 months (depends on usage) because they are highly reactive metal. A layer of tin prevents direct contact of food and moisture with copper/brass, which ultimately prevents you from food poisoning.

2. Natural earthen pots of Clay

Ayurveda suggests cooking in clay because it improves the quality of food and retains nutrients while cooking by leeching healthy minerals such as calcium, magnesium, and iron from clay.

As cooking in clay pots is a slow process because of the high porosity and uniform distribution of heat throughout the body. Oil requirement is fairly less, in comparison to cooking in another type of material.

A word of caution: Avoid glazed pots, which may leech lead or cadmium while cooking and contaminate your food, and of course, Handle with Care!

Safe, Non-toxic, and less-reactive cookware

3. Almighty, Cast Iron

Cast Iron is a durable, sturdy, and heavy-weight metal that is here to stay long. Though, it does not get heated evenly but, it retains heat for a long time.

If it is properly seasoned, it may allow you to cook acidic foods for a very short time and thus, avoid long-simmered acidic recipes such as tomato sauce. Foods cooked in this type of cookware, increase the amount of iron in your food, especially with high moisture content, which can help to reduce Iron deficiency as well!

Another word of caution: Don’t let your cast iron utensil stay wet, otherwise it will get rusty! Season it with oil to retain the non-stick property.

4. 304 (18/8) and FW2 (Nickel-free) Stainless steel

Stainless steel is one of the commonly used and popular choices among consumers looking for non-toxic cookware. 18/8 steel is an alloy of 18% chromium and 8% nickel and offers better resistance to corrosion and is non-reactive.

Nickel-free stainless steel is better than 304 grade, developed by Nippon Steel & Sumikin Stainless (NSSC) known as NSSC-FW2, or simply FW2. It contains 0.3% tin, 16% chrome, and no nickel, molybdenum, and copper content.
